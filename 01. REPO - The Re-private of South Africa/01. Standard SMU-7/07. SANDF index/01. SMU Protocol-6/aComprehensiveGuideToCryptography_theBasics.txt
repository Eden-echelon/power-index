A Comprehensive Guide to Cryptography: the Basics

Cryptography is the mathematical science of securing information so that only authorized parties can access it. It is the foundation of digital trust.

The Six Major Security Goals -

To understand cryptography, one must first understand what it aims to achieve.

01.  confidentiality: the most well-known goal. It ensures that only intended recipients can read the data. even if an attacker intercepts the message, it should be unintelligible.

02.  integrity: this guarantees that data has not been altered in transit, whether accidentally or maliciously. the recipient can verify that the message received is exactly what was sent.

03.  authentication: this allows a party to verify the identity of the sender or receiver. it confirms that you are indeed communicating with who you think you are.

04.  non-repudiation: this prevents a sender from denying they sent a message. it provides undeniable proof of the origin of the data.

05.  subtlety (covertness): in many threat models, especially in high-risk environments, the *act of communicating* can be as dangerous as the message itself.

    - the concept: it's not just about protecting the message; it's about protecting the existence of the transmission. even with unbreakable encryption, an adversary can perform traffic analysis—examining the volume, timing, and metadata of your data flows.

    - why text matters: a massive spike in data, like an image or video upload, is a tactical giveaway. Text is the most subtle medium. It minimizes noise and reduces the digital footprint, helping to hide the fact that a secret conversation is even taking place.

06.  availability: a system, its' data, and its cryptographic services must be accessible to authorized users when needed. A denial-of-service attack, for instance, targets availability, even if the data itself remains confidential and unaltered.

---

Core Concepts

- encryption: The process of transforming readable data (**plaintext**) into an unreadable, scrambled format (**ciphertext**) using a mathematical algorithm and a secret value.

- decryption: The reverse process of transforming ciphertext back into plaintext using a key.

- key: a secret value (a string of bits) that controls the encryption and decryption process. a fundamental principle of modern cryptography is **Kerckhoffs’s Principle:** a cryptosystem should be secure even if everything about the system, except the key, is public knowledge.* security depends entirely on the secrecy of the key.

---

Types of Cryptography

There are three fundamental types of cryptographic algorithms, each serving a distinct purpose.

A) Symmetric Cryptography

- mechanism: uses the *same secret key* for both encryption and decryption.
- advantages: It is *very fast* and efficient for encrypting large amounts of data.
- disadvantage: The main challenge is **key distribution**. The sender and receiver must securely share the single secret key without anyone else intercepting it.

- examples:
    - Advanced Encryption Standard (AES): The modern gold standard, widely used today.
    - Data Encryption Standard (DES): The predecessor, now considered obsolete due to its small key size.
- Use Cases: Disk encryption (e.g., BitLocker, FileVault), encrypting files, securing VPN tunnels.

B) Asymmetric Cryptography (Public-Key Cryptography)

- mechanism: uses a mathematically linked *key pair*:

    - A *Public Key:* Can be shared openly with anyone.
    - A *Private Key:* Must be kept absolutely secret by its owner.

- *Advantages:* It elegantly *solves the key distribution problem*. Anyone can use your public key to encrypt a message that *only you* can decrypt with your private key.
- *Disadvantage:* It is *significantly slower* than symmetric cryptography.

- *Examples:*
    - *RSA:* One of the first and most widely implemented public-key algorithms.
    - *Elliptic Curve Cryptography (ECC):* A modern alternative that provides equivalent security with smaller keys, making it more efficient.
- *Use Cases:* Securing the initial handshake in **HTTPS (TLS)** , secure email (PGP), and creating **digital signatures**.

C) Hash Functions

- Mechanism: These are *one-way mathematical functions*. They take an input (plaintext) of any size and produce a fixed-size output called a *hash*, *digest*, or *fingerprint*.

- Key Properties:

    - One-way: It is computationally infeasible to reverse a hash back to the original input.
    - Deterministic: The same input will always produce the same hash.
    - Collision-resistant: It is infeasible to find two different inputs that produce the same hash.

- Advantages: They are used to verify **integrity**—any change to the data, even a single bit, will result in a completely different hash.

- Examples:
    - SHA-256 (Secure Hash Algorithm): A widely used, secure hash function.
    - MD5 (Message Digest 5): A popular but now **broken** algorithm, as collisions have been found. It should not be used for security.
- Use Cases: Verifying file downloads, storing password hashes (never plaintext passwords), and ensuring data integrity in digital signatures.

How Modern Security Actually Works: The Hybrid Approach

In real-world systems, no single type of cryptography is used in isolation. They are combined to leverage the strengths of each. This is often called a *hybrid cryptosystem*.

Example: HTTPS (The foundation of secure web browsing)

1.  Key Exchange (Asymmetric): When your browser connects to a secure website (e.g., a bank), it uses asymmetric cryptography (like RSA or ECC) to perform a **TLS handshake**. This securely exchanges a temporary, unique **session key** without exposing it to eavesdroppers.

2.  Bulk Encryption (Symmetric): Once the session key is established, all data for the rest of the session (the web page content, form data, etc.) is encrypted using **symmetric cryptography** (like AES). This provides the speed needed for the large amount of data being transferred.

3.  Integrity (Hash Functions): Throughout the process, hash functions (like SHA-256) are used to create Message Authentication Codes (MACs). These ensure that the data has not been tampered with during transmission.

This hybrid system gives us the best of both worlds: the secure key exchange of asymmetric crypto combined with the speed and efficiency of symmetric crypto, all verified for integrity by hash functions.

---

The Most Important Truth

Without keys, there is no cryptography. Without proper key management, there is no security.

You can have the most advanced algorithms in the world, but if your keys are:
- Too short and easy to guess,
- Stored insecurely (e.g., hardcoded in source code),
- Shared improperly,
- Or not revoked when compromised,

...then your entire security infrastructure is an illusion. **Key management**—the secure generation, storage, distribution, rotation, and destruction of keys—is the single most critical and challenging aspect of applied cryptography.
